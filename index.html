Alexander Grothendieck

Introduction

Although mathematics became more and more abstract and general throughout the 20th century, it was Alexander Grothendieck who was the greatest master of this trend. His unique skill was to eliminate all unnecessary hypotheses and burrow into an area so deeply that its inner patterns on the most abstract level revealed themselves -- and then, like a magician, show how the solution of old problems fell out in straightforward ways now that their real nature had been revealed. His strength and intensity were legendary. He worked long hours, transforming totally the field of algebraic geometry and its connections with algebraic number theory. He was considered by many the greatest mathematician of the 20th century.

--David Mumford and John Tate

Here is a timeline of Alexander Gronthendieck's life:

Childhood and Youth

  • 1928-1933 Alexander Grothendieck was born on 28 March 1928 in Berlin, where he lived with his parents Johanna Grothendieck and Alexander Schapiro, and his sister Maidi (Hanka's daughter from an earlier marriage). His father worked as a street photographer, one of the only jobs his anarchist beliefs allowed him, and his mother as a journalist.
  • 1933-1939 Grothendieck parents leaved Berlin for Paris to evade Nazism, leaving Grothendieck with a foster family, the Heydorns, in Hamburg. There he went to school and then Gymnasium, while his parents took part in the Spanish civil war.
  • 1939-1942 Due to the incoming war, foster mother sent Grothendieck to his parents in France. Grothendieck and his mother are interned in Rieucros Camp as "undesirable" while his father is sent to Le Vernet, later being sent to Auschwitz where he died.
  • 1942-1945 His mother is sent to Gurs while he is permitted to live in the village Le Chambon sur Lignon. There he attended the famous Collège-Lyceé Cévénol through his baccalauréat, where he first became fascinated with mathematics.

Mathematical Career

  • 1945-1949 After the war, Grothendieck and his mother moved to a small village near Montpellier where he worked on a farm while studying mathematics at the University of Montpellier. After graduating in 1948, he went to Paris where he took several courses and met most of the famous mathematicians of the time.
  • 1949-1953 Grothendieck worked on his doctorate under the supervision of Laurent Schwartz, introducing new concepts and obtaining several results in the area of functional analysis. By the end of his doctorate he was considered one of the leading figures in the area.
  • 1953-1958 After he doctorated, Grothendieck worked as a visiting researcher at the University of São Paulo, University of Kansas in Lawrence and CNRS. In this period he migrated from functionl analysis to algebraic geometry, publishing revolutionary works on homologiacal algebra and the proof of the Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch theorem.
  • 1958-1970 The IHES is created in order for Grothendieck to have a research position there. During the twelve years he spent there, he completely renewed the theory of algebraic geometry, writing Éléments de Géométrie Algébrique (with Dieudonné) and Séminaire de Géométrie Algébrique (with his students). It is during this time, in 1966, that he is awarded the Fields Medal, however, for political reasons, he refused to travel to Moscow for the ceremony.

Turning Point and Later Works

  • 1970-1973 Suddenly, on discovering that the IHES is partly funded by the miltary, Grothendieck left the institute. He became acutely interested in ecological causes and founded the group Survivre et Vivre.
  • 1973-1980 Grothendieck accepted a professorship at the University of Montpellier. There he lectured and had some graduate students, but got discouraged by the teaching situation and became increasingly estranged from the mathematical community. It was during this time that he started meditating.
  • 1980-1988 During this period, Grothendieck wrote thousands of pages of mathematical and non-mathematical meditations. In 1984, he applied for a temporary position with the CNRS with his proposal for research Esquisse d'un Programme, a program that continues to date to be of major interest in several branches of advanced mathematics. In 1988, Grothendieck was awarded (with his student Pierre Deligne) the Crafoord Prize, but refused it.
  • 1988-2014 Grothendieck officially retired on January 10, 1988. Later, in 1991, he left his home for an unknown location. He spent his time writing an enormous work on physics and philosophical meditations on themes such as free choice, determinism and the existence of evil. On 13 November 2014, aged 86, Grothendieck died in the hospital of Saint-Girons, Ariège.
